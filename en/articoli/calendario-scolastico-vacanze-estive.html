School and calendar

Italian schools close for too long in summer

The fact that schools close for about three months in summer is often treated as immovable. In practice it depends on past organisational choices that can be changed with political will, planning and investment. A different distribution of breaks could improve learning, family organisation, female employment, the use of school buildings and the reduction of inequalities between students.

Summer holidays in the European comparison

The right source for comparing European school calendars is Eurydice, School calendars in Europe. Eurydice compares calendars in 39 European countries and makes it possible to observe the duration and frequency of school breaks. Annual instruction time can also be checked with Eurydice, Recommended Annual Instruction Time 2024/2025 and OECD, Education at a Glance 2024.

Italy concentrates a very large share of school breaks between June and September. Other European countries organise the calendar differently. The summer break is shorter and a larger share of holidays is distributed during the school year. Rest days do not have to disappear. They can be distributed better.

In the data used for this article, the Italian break between June and September is about 99 days. Spain is around eleven weeks, France around eight, and Germany, England and the Netherlands around six. These values should be read as orders of magnitude, because calendars vary by year, territory and school level.

Why such a long break creates inequalities

Three months without school are not the same for everyone. One student can spend the summer with books, sports, courses, languages, travel, camps, present parents and tutors. Another can spend weeks without structured activities, adequate spaces, study support or formative experiences. After such a long break, these differences accumulate.

During the year, school reduces at least part of the dependence on family resources. When school closes for a very long period, this function weakens. The quality of the summer depends much more on income, parental time, cultural capital, family networks and the local supply of services.

A long break also affects family organisation. Three months of closure require holidays, grandparents, summer centres, babysitters, flexible work or sacrifices. In Italy this burden is still often distributed unevenly, with a greater impact on mothers and on the second earner. A more balanced distribution would reduce one of the most difficult organisational peaks of the year.

Summer learning loss

Summer learning loss has been studied for decades, especially in the US literature. The basic idea is that a long summer interruption can produce loss of skills or a slowdown in learning, with stronger effects for students who have less access to educational activities during the summer. The meta-analysis by Cooper, Nye, Charlton, Lindsay and Greathouse found average losses during the summer, more evident in mathematics. Alexander, Entwisle and Olson linked summer trajectories to social inequalities accumulated over time.

In recent years, part of the literature has scaled down some of the stronger conclusions. Paul von Hippel showed that some historical estimates depend heavily on the tests used and on measurement choices. Downey, von Hippel and Broh distinguish the role of school, family and measurement problems in learning gaps.

This does not remove the problem. Even when the average loss is less clear than previously thought, the summer break remains very different across families. Some children take courses, do sports, travel, learn English, attend camps and structured activities. Others have access to nothing comparable. A long summer closure increases the period in which opportunities depend almost entirely on family resources. In this sense the school calendar can widen inequalities even when the precise measurement of learning loss is debated.

Heat and schools

The objection about heat is often used to close the discussion. Spain has hot summers and a shorter summer break than Italy. This does not mean copying Spain. It means that heat, calendar and investment need to be treated together.

Italian schools already operate in hot periods. Upper-secondary final exams take place between June and July. Recovery activities and make-up exams are held in the summer. Heatwaves can arrive in May, June and late September. The issue of school buildings already exists within the current calendar.

Even without changing the calendar, investment in ventilation, thermal mitigation and air conditioning would probably be needed. Extreme temperatures make classrooms less usable, worsen study conditions and make it harder to manage exams, recovery activities and the beginning and end of the school year.

The heat dashboard uses the MIM dataset on school facilities. The direct field is CONDIZIONAMENTOVENTILAZIONE. Out of 60,030 school-building rows, 4,457 indicate the presence of air conditioning or ventilation, 36,214 indicate absence and 19,359 have no defined value. Overall, declared presence is 7.42%, declared absence 60.33% and undefined cases 32.25%.

How costs and timing are estimated

Costs are not estimated by multiplying a standard price by the number of classrooms. The model starts from MIM data to identify missing services, weaknesses and priorities. It then uses OpenCoesione benchmarks to estimate costs and timing on the basis of comparable public interventions. Projects already financed and monitored, including interventions linked to EU funds, cohesion funds and national programmes when present in the data, make it possible to observe how much similar interventions cost and how long they took.

The mean sums all values and divides them by the number of observations. The median is the central value of the distribution: half of the observed projects are below that value and half are above it. The median is useful when there are extreme values, because a few very costly or very slow projects can shift the mean substantially. This often happens in public works. For this reason the model looks at the distribution, not only at an average value.

For thermal comfort, the benchmark uses 37 comparable projects. The minimum observed cost is about EUR 14,600, the median cost about EUR 50,000 and the maximum cost EUR 500,000. The minimum historical duration is 1 month, the median duration 4.37 months and the maximum duration 20.66 months. For calendar-related heat mitigation, the benchmark uses 152 comparable projects. The minimum observed cost is EUR 610, the median cost about EUR 46,800 and the maximum cost about EUR 1.18 million. The minimum historical duration is 1 month, the median duration 4.27 months and the maximum duration 80.45 months.

To transform these benchmarks into a national timetable, the model uses an explicit rule. It starts from the median duration of OpenCoesione projects, converts it into years by rounding up, adds one administrative year for review, design, procurement and coordination, then compares the result with the minimum programme duration set in the scenario. For thermal comfort and heat mitigation, the minimum programme duration is three years. A three-year duration is therefore not the median duration of a single construction site. It is the national baseline duration after the prudential programme constraint.

The tail of the distribution is used to control risk. In the prudent case, the model uses the 95th percentile of historical durations and adds two administrative years. For thermal comfort, this leads to about four years. For calendar-related heat mitigation, it leads to about five years. The three-year baseline duration is therefore a prudent estimate for the national programme, not a guess detached from the data.

The estimate is prudent. In the model there are buildings that could already be closed or merged today for demographic reasons, low student numbers, weak structural conditions or organisational inefficiency. If part of these buildings were actually closed, reused or replaced by better sites, adaptation costs could be lower.

Teachers’ holidays and temporary contracts

Redistributing the calendar does not mean reducing teachers’ holidays. It means changing their placement during the year. The total number of days does not have to decrease.

The calendar also affects temporary teachers. Today many temporary contracts end in June and some teachers spend the summer without a salary, relying on NASpI until school activities resume. A different distribution of the calendar does not solve precarious employment by itself, but it reduces the organisational discontinuity of a system that currently concentrates school closures, contract endings and the annual restart at the same point of the year.

Social benefits

A different distribution of the calendar would reduce the long summer discontinuity and the period in which opportunities depend almost entirely on family resources. It would make family organisation more manageable, especially where informal networks, available grandparents or affordable summer services are missing.

A less concentrated calendar would also reduce the summer peak in holidays, tourism, transport and services. Schools are central public infrastructure in local areas. Investing to make them usable for longer increases the social return of buildings that today remain unused for a very large part of the year.

The climate-related benefit exists even regardless of calendar reform. Ventilation, shading, insulation, thermal mitigation and air conditioning are needed anyway, because intense heat also affects May, June and September.

Conclusion

The roughly three-month summer closure is not destiny. It is an organisational choice inherited from the past. It can be changed with data, investment and planning.

The point is not to take rest away from students and teachers. The point is to stop concentrating almost all rest in summer and build a calendar that is better suited to the country as it is today.
